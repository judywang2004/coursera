                            Adam Wasserman c05m0 • 7 months ago

                            I have to disagree. As someone who was developing in 1984, my feeling is there was just as much to know back then as there is today; it was simply lower level is all.

                            These days I would consider a well-rounded developer someone who has (amongst many other criteria) worked with both Javascript and Cx/Java, who has an awareness of the relative strengths and weaknesses of frameworks (Angular vs. Meteor for example).

                            Back then it would have been someone who knew how to profile their code and then optimize inner loops in assembly, someone who knew what the A5 register did on a Motorola 68k in MacOS, and how to use it, someone who knew how to XOR instead of a using a buffer update.

                            When the items in the stack were fewer and more primative, we had to go deeper, and the information was a lot harder to come by then these days when all you have to do is fire up a web browser.

                            No matter what you call it, there has always been a need for programmers that have an end-to-end awareness of their craft, from the business needs through to what shows up on a screen, and everything in-between.

                            This type of programmer has always been a rare beast because of the breadth and depth of knowledge required to do a good job. The focus has shifted from machine language to frameworks, but it is no more or less demanding than it ever was.

                                                                                The key thing to take away from this is "more things about related areas" which makes you a master at a higher abstract of those combined areas, and therefore more of a philosopher or scientist depending on the nature of how you can apply your knowledge.

                                                                                Ask yourself: first what do you know can be done, second do you also know how to go about doing it, third can you demonstrate it, and finally, how deep can you explain what is going on with the underlying areas of this higher level abstraction?
                                                                                It's ok to be a jack of all trades, but unfortunately people that hire are often not as knowledgable as those who accumulate knowledge in surrounding areas of a specific specialty, and seek to filter out candidates who believe to be able to reach some (often nonsensical but other times attainable) criteria.
                                                                                I'm glad you can see where you can specialize. As a front end or back end developer I think those are 2 abstractions worth aspiring towards, and don't deny that there is certainly crossover between the two diciplines. I'd like to see more mentorship opportunities that would help separate would-be-full-stack-developers into one of these two areas as I think it would really show where personal interests lie, and finding others to work with, collaborate with, and generate innovative projects would be a process of seeking your other half instead of a self-proclaimed "know-it-all" who may not even meet the requirements of a recruiter (and thus further skew the expectation of what a "full stack" developer even is).

                                                                                                                                          Kurt Kemple • a year ago

                                                                                                                                          I think one of the main points being overlooked here is that "full-stack" developers have a significant advantage when it comes to understanding the application from a macro level. They understand how each part of the stack works in regard to the application as a whole.

                                                                                                                                          While they may not know the intricate details of Java or any other language they understand why and how it is being used. There is one job that will probably pay way more than any "specialized" development job and this the application architect. It is imperative that the architect be knowledgable in all areas but a master in either one or none.

                                                                                                                                          It is not the architect's job to know the finite details of the implementation of each level of the stack, only to offer guidance on framework choices and application architectural patterns, and keep developers informed and ensure adherence to the technical specifications.

                                                                                                                                          Without having come from a background where you have experience in each of these levels of the stack you can't accurately make suggestions on the architecture of an application, whether it be desktop software or a web application.

                                                                                                                                          To say that it is better to specialize in a single area than to amass knowledge and skill in multiple areas just points out who will have better success in a developer role (micro or specialized), and who will do better in an architectural role (macro or "full-stack").

                                                                                                                                          So with that be happy to be a full-stack developer because it will open many doors for you should ever decide to solve puzzles at the macro level and not the micro level.

                                                                                                                                          Cheers and happy coding!

                                                                                                                                                  Software engineering best practices are not bound to a technology. Technology best practices are bound to a technology. Once you become a good software engineer you can learn languages and their best practices a lot easier as you understand the problems these languages are trying to solve, and the complexity they are abstracting away for you.

                                                                                                                                                  Once you can clearly understand these things moving around on the stack becomes easier, and in a world where we are moving to developers owning the full life-cycle of a service/product. We will see less and less of a distinction between a full-stack developer and a developer.

                                                                                                                                                  As to a lot of the other stuff you mentioned, I'm not sure how pressure to complete work weighs in to this discussion, or how it can make or break you as a software engineer. Only you can make or break yourself as a full-stack engineer.

                                                                                                                                                  Although I will say that if you are working in a place where research and prototyping to find the best solution is not a common practice then you should move on. There is nothing worse for your future as a developer than a bad working environment.

                                                                                                                                                  Also I am not saying you can call yourself an architect and learn as you go. It requires experience before making architectural decisions.

                                                                                                                                                  I agree that the term full-stack is not a great one, but it is what software engineers who work in different environments are referred to. As we lose the idea of a "stack" (which is happening right now, look at the differences between a monolith architecture and a microservices architecture) we will lose the idea that developers must focus on one language/stack of a system.
